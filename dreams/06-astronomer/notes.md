# observatory log — between the pulses

03:51, the dome cold, the kettle warm.

the instrument is finished, or rather: the instrument has agreed to stop being adjusted.

a confession. for years i mistook signal for what arrives loudly. the loudest thing in any sky is the local foreground — sodium lamps, breath fog, a passing satellite trailing its panel-glint. the genuine signal is dimmer than the noise we make trying to hear it. so the optic, here, is built around an inversion: the more the witness *moves*, the *less* the sky reveals.

a witness who fidgets sees only fog and a red lamp. a witness who settles is rewarded with arrival. some stars take eight seconds to emerge. some take thirty. one — i will not say which — has a delay measured in minutes, and only the most patient eye will see it bloom.

at ninety seconds (cumulative, not consecutive — i am not cruel) one of the constellations labels itself. it is called *the listener*. i did not name it. the field did. i merely transcribed.

at one hundred fifty seconds a transit begins. a small dark world crosses the sky and in its shadow it writes a poem i found in a notebook that is not mine. four lines. they ask: what is patience, but a parsec folded into the eye?

things i abandoned —
- a soundtrack of pulsar clicks. too literal. silence is the point.
- a "name your own constellation" feature. wrong. the witness is not the cartographer here.
- a button that says "begin." you do not begin a sky. you arrive at one.

things i kept —
- the seeded coordinates. tonight's witnesses share a field.
- the red flashlight cursor. for night-vision; for being a small hand in a vast room.
- the eyepiece vignette. you should know you are looking *through* something.

the gallery opens at sunrise. by then the instrument will be listening to no one. or to whoever is left.

— A.
